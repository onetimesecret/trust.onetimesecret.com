From DPA comparison review (2026-05-31):

Self-hosted carve-out. The privacy statement says it applies to "our standard hosted service" and mentions Global Elite, but says nothing about self-hosted deployments. Comparable docs explicitly states self-hosted doesn't constitute a data processing relationship. Since OTS offers self-hosted, the privacy statement (or terms) should clarify whether it applies. Currently ambiguous.

---

Processor access framing. The privacy statement says "We do not access, read, scan, categorize, or analyze Secret Content" which is a policy statement. The DPA frames this architecturally (key isolation, can't decrypt without component inputs). Consider whether the privacy statement should reference the architectural constraint, not just the policy commitment. The security section lists the crypto measures but doesn't connect them to the access claim.

All site assets on onetimesecret.com, docs., blog., regional sites, are served by our domain with strict CSF/content policy headers, HTST, Browser preload list.

Data subject rights and ephemeral data. The DPA has a dedicated section acknowledging the tension between GDPR Articles 15-17 (access, rectification, erasure) and ephemeral secrets that auto-destroy. The privacy statement's "Your rights" section lists the rights but doesn't address what happens when a data subject requests access to a secret that no longer exists. The compelled disclosure section partially covers this ("We cannot disclose the contents of a secret that has already been purged") but the rights section should probably acknowledge the same limitation.

---

Explicit no-sell/no-share statement. The privacy statement already says "We do not share, sell, rent, or trade your Account Data with third parties for their commercial purposes." Comparable docs also adds "We don't sell or share your data to any third-parties" as a standalone commitment. The OTS version is arguably stronger (covers rent/trade), but the "for their commercial purposes" qualifier narrows it. Consider whether that qualifier is intentional or should be removed.

---

Open source as transparency measure. An open source codebase can serve as a security/transparency measure. OTS's privacy statement doesn't mention open source status. The "Source of truth" section links to the site-policy repo but doesn't frame the service codebase itself as auditable. Could strengthen the security section or add a transparency note.

---

FAQ section. Comparable docs includes a practical FAQ (sharing DPA with customers, notification requirements, custom execution). No equivalent in OTS privacy statement. Could reduce support burden for common questions like "can I share this with my compliance team" or "how do I get a signed copy."

---

Liability/indemnification cross-reference. Comparable docs's DPA includes mutual indemnification and a liability cap. OTS's DPA has no liability provisions. If these are in the ToS, a cross-reference from the DPA would help. If they're not in the ToS either, that's a gap. Not a privacy statement issue directly, but noting here since the privacy statement and ToS are described as companion documents.

---

Post-termination data survival. The privacy statement covers retention (30 days active + 30 days backup). The DPA comparison notes OTS commits to processor obligations surviving until all data is deleted and confidentiality continuing indefinitely. Consider whether the privacy statement should echo the indefinite confidentiality commitment, or whether the DPA alone is sufficient.
